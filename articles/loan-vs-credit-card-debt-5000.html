Loan vs. Credit Card: Which Is Cheaper for $5,000 Debt?

Published: March 18, 2026 | Est. read time: 8 minutes

🎯 The short answer: A personal loan is almost always cheaper for $5,000+ debt if you have fair credit (580+). Credit cards only win if you can pay off the balance in 3–6 months.

The $5,000 Debt Scenario

You have $5,000 in credit card debt. You're paying high interest. You want to know: Should you get a personal loan to pay it off, or just pay down the credit card directly?

This is one of the most common financial questions. The answer depends on your credit score, how fast you can pay, and the interest rates available to you.

When a Credit Card Is Better (Yes, Sometimes)

✓ Credit card wins if:

  • You can pay off the balance in under 6 months – The interest difference is minimal, and you avoid a hard inquiry from a loan application
  • You have a 0% APR balance transfer offer – Many cards offer 12–21 months at 0% APR (3–5% transfer fee). This beats any personal loan.
  • Your credit score is below 580 – Loan APRs would be 30%+, which is worse than a credit card's 22–25%
  • You need flexibility – Credit cards let you pay more or less each month. Loans have fixed payments.

✓ Personal loan wins if:

  • You need 12+ months to pay off the debt – The interest savings add up significantly
  • You have fair credit (580–669) – Loan APRs (10–20%) are typically lower than credit card APRs (20–30%)
  • You want a fixed end date – Loans force you to pay off debt. Credit cards let you fall back into minimum payments.
  • You're consolidating multiple credit cards – One payment instead of 3–4 is easier to manage

The 0% Balance Transfer Hack (Best Option If You Qualify)

If you have good credit (670+), a 0% APR balance transfer card is better than both loans and regular credit cards.

How it works:

  1. Apply for a card with 0% intro APR on balance transfers (12–21 months)
  2. Pay a one-time transfer fee (3–5%, typically $75–$250 on $5,000)
  3. Transfer your $5,000 credit card balance
  4. Pay $250–$400 per month for 12–18 months – $0 interest

Example with $5,000, 5% transfer fee ($250), 15 months at 0% APR:

  • Monthly payment to pay off before interest starts: $350
  • Total cost: $5,250 (just the fee)
  • Savings vs. personal loan: ~$1,000
  • Savings vs. credit card minimum: ~$1,500
⚠️ Warning: If you don't pay off the full balance before the 0% period ends, you'll owe retroactive interest on the remaining balance at 20–30% APR. Mark your calendar.

Step-by-Step: How to Decide for Your Situation

  1. Calculate your credit card payoff time – Use the calculator above. Enter your current balance, APR, and what you can pay monthly.
  2. Get loan pre-approvals – Check lenders like Upgrade and Avant with soft pulls. Note the APR and terms offered.
  3. Run the loan scenario – Use our calculator with the loan's APR and a term equal to or shorter than your credit card payoff time.
  4. Compare total costs – Choose whichever is lower.
  5. Check 0% balance transfer offers – If your credit score is 670+, this likely beats both options.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Extending your loan term to lower the payment

This increases total interest. A 5-year loan on $5,000 at 15% costs $2,140 in interest – more than most credit cards.

❌ Taking a loan with an origination fee over 5%

A $5,000 loan with an 8% fee means you only receive $4,600 but owe $5,000. That's like paying 8% interest upfront. See our no-fee loan guide.

❌ Running up the credit card again after paying it off

The #1 mistake: people pay off their credit card with a loan, then max out the card again. Now they have loan debt + new credit card debt. Close the card or lower the limit after paying it off.

❌ Missing a loan payment

A single missed payment destroys your credit score (50–100 point drop). Set up autopay immediately.

FAQs

Will a personal loan lower my credit score?

Temporarily, yes (hard inquiry: -5 to -10 points; new account: -5 points). But paying off your credit card (lowering utilization from 80% to 0%) typically adds 30–80 points. Net positive.

Can I use a personal loan to pay off credit card debt from the same bank?

Yes, but some banks restrict this. Ask before applying. Most allow it as long as you're not using a loan from the same bank to pay that same bank's credit card.

What if I have multiple credit cards with $5,000 total debt?

A personal loan simplifies everything – one payment instead of 3–4. The math is the same: compare total interest costs.

Should I close my credit card after paying it off with a loan?

No – closing a card reduces your available credit, which increases utilization on other cards. Keep it open but don't use it. Or use it for one small purchase per month and pay in full.

Final Verdict

For most people with $5,000+ credit card debt and fair credit (580–669): A 2–3 year personal loan at 10–18% APR is cheaper than paying the credit card minimum. You'll save $300–$800 in interest.

But first, check if you qualify for a 0% balance transfer card. If you have good credit (670+), that's the best option by far.

If you can pay off the debt in under 6 months, stick with the credit card and avoid the hard inquiry.
