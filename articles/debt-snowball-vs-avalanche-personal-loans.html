Debt Snowball vs. Avalanche With Personal Loans – Which Is Faster?

Published: March 19, 2026 | Est. read time: 8 minutes

🧠 The short answer: Avalanche saves more money (mathematically best). Snowball keeps you motivated (psychologically best). Pick the one you'll stick with.

The Two Methods Explained

Debt Snowball (Dave Ramsey Method)

Pay off debts from smallest balance to largest, regardless of interest rate. Pay minimum on all debts, put all extra money toward the smallest debt. When it's gone, roll that payment to the next smallest.

Pros: Quick wins keep you motivated. Studies show higher success rates.

Cons: May pay more total interest if a high-interest debt is large.

Debt Avalanche (Mathematically Optimal)

Pay off debts from highest interest rate to lowest, regardless of balance. Put all extra money toward the debt costing you the most per month.

Pros: Saves the most money. Pays off total debt faster.

Cons: Slow initial progress if high-interest debt is large. Less motivating.

Real Example: $15,000 Total Debt

Debt portfolio:

  • Personal Loan A: $2,000 at 10% APR
  • Credit Card B: $5,000 at 22% APR
  • Personal Loan C: $8,000 at 15% APR

Assumptions: $800 total monthly payment (minimums ~$450, extra $350)

Debt Snowball (Smallest to Largest Balance)

  1. Pay off Loan A ($2,000 at 10%) – takes ~3 months
  2. Then Credit Card B ($5,000 at 22%) – takes ~7 more months
  3. Then Loan C ($8,000 at 15%) – takes ~10 more months

Total time: ~20 months | Total interest: ~$1,850

Debt Avalanche (Highest to Lowest Interest)

  1. Pay off Credit Card B ($5,000 at 22%) – takes ~6 months
  2. Then Loan C ($8,000 at 15%) – takes ~9 more months
  3. Then Loan A ($2,000 at 10%) – takes ~2 more months

Total time: ~17 months | Total interest: ~$1,450

💰 Avalanche saves $400 and 3 months over Snowball in this example. But Snowball gives you a "win" (paying off Loan A) in just 3 months, which keeps many people going.

Which Method Is Right for You?

Choose Debt Snowball If:

  • ✓ You need motivation wins to stay on track
  • ✓ You've tried and failed at debt payoff before
  • ✓ Your debts are similar in size (the order doesn't matter much)
  • ✓ You have one very small debt (under $1,000) to knock out quickly

Choose Debt Avalanche If:

  • ✓ You're disciplined and don't need emotional wins
  • ✓ You have a high-interest debt (20%+ credit card)
  • ✓ Your debts have very different interest rates (10% vs. 25%)
  • ✓ You're mathematically minded and want to minimize total cost

How Personal Loans Fit Into Both Strategies

A personal loan can be either a tool or an obstacle:

Using a Personal Loan FOR Debt Payoff (Consolidation)

You take one personal loan to pay off multiple high-interest debts (credit cards, payday loans). Then you have ONE debt to attack with snowball or avalanche.

Example: Instead of 3 debts (credit card 25%, store card 22%, personal loan 18%), you get one consolidation loan at 15%. Now you only have one debt—no snowball vs. avalanche decision needed.

See our guide: Loan vs. Credit Card for Debt Payoff

Personal Loan AS a Debt (If You Already Have One)

If you already have a personal loan, treat it like any other debt in your snowball or avalanche order. Compare its balance and APR to your other debts.

Real Success Stories

Debts: $15k credit card (24%), $10k car loan (7%), $5k student loan (4%). Monthly budget $1,200. Avalanche focused on credit card first. Paid off in 14 months. Total interest $3,100. Snowball would have cost $5,400 interest (+$2,300).

Case 2: Mike (Snowball) – Stayed Motivated

Debts: $500 medical bill (0%), $3k credit card (18%), $12k personal loan (12%). Monthly budget $600. Mike paid off the $500 medical bill in 1 month, felt motivated, and completed all debts in 22 months. He says he would have quit if he'd started with the $12k loan.

📊 Statistic: A Harvard Business School study found that debt snowball users are 20–30% more likely to successfully eliminate all debt than avalanche users – even though avalanche saves more money mathematically.

Step-by-Step: Start Paying Today

  1. List all debts – Balance, APR, minimum payment
  2. Determine your total monthly payment – Minimums + extra amount
  3. Choose snowball or avalanche – Be honest about your personality
  4. Use the calculator above to see estimated payoff time
  5. Set up autopay for minimums – Never miss a payment
  6. Put all extra toward the first target debt
  7. Celebrate each debt elimination – Small rewards keep momentum

FAQs

Can I switch methods halfway?

Yes. Many people start with snowball for motivation, then switch to avalanche once they have momentum. The important thing is to keep paying.

Should I include my mortgage in snowball/avalanche?

Typically no – mortgages are separate because of their size and tax treatment. Focus on consumer debt (credit cards, personal loans, car loans) first.

What about 0% APR debts?

Pay only the minimum on 0% debts until the promo period ends. Then prioritize them before they start accruing interest at 20–30%.

How does a personal loan consolidation affect my credit score?

Initial drop (hard inquiry + new account): -10 to -20 points. But paying off multiple credit cards (lowering utilization) typically adds 30–80 points. Net positive within 3–6 months.

Final Recommendation

If you're highly disciplined and want to save every dollar: Use debt avalanche. Prioritize by APR. You'll pay the least interest.

If you've struggled with debt payoff before: Use debt snowball. Knock out the smallest balance first. The quick wins will keep you going.

Either way: The best method is the one you'll actually follow. Starting is more important than optimizing.
