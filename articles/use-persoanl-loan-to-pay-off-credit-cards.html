Can You Use a Personal Loan to Pay Off Credit Cards? (2026 Guide)

Published: March 25, 2026 | Est. read time: 9 minutes

💳 The short answer: Yes – and it's often a smart financial move. Replacing 22–30% credit card APR with 10–18% personal loan APR can save you thousands in interest.

How Personal Loan Debt Consolidation Works

The process in 4 steps:

  1. You apply for a personal loan (typically $5,000–$50,000).
  2. The lender approves you based on credit, income, and debt-to-income ratio.
  3. The lender sends you the loan funds (or pays credit card companies directly).
  4. You use the funds to pay off your credit card balances in full.
  5. Now you have one monthly payment (the loan) instead of multiple credit card payments.
📊 Key benefit: Personal loan APRs are typically 10–20 percentage points lower than credit card APRs for fair credit borrowers (580–669).

Real Dollar Example: $15,000 Credit Card Debt

When Debt Consolidation Makes Sense

✓ Good reasons to consolidate:

  • Your credit score has improved – You opened cards at 22% APR when your score was 620. Now it's 680, and you qualify for 12% APR.
  • You have multiple cards (3+) – Managing multiple due dates is stressful. One payment is simpler.
  • You're paying minimums only – At 24% APR with minimum payments, you'll pay for 10+ years. A 3-year loan forces payoff.
  • You have a fixed budget – Loan payments are predictable. Credit card minimums fluctuate with balance.

✗ Bad reasons to consolidate:

  • You haven't fixed spending habits – If you pay off cards with a loan then max them out again, you'll have loan debt + new credit card debt. This is the #1 mistake.
  • The loan APR is higher than your cards – Rare, but possible if your credit has worsened. Don't consolidate to a higher rate.
  • You're close to paying off cards anyway – If you'll pay off credit cards in 6–12 months, the interest savings from a loan may not outweigh the origination fee.

How Debt Consolidation Affects Your Credit Score

Short-term impact (first 1–3 months): -10 to -20 points

  • Hard inquiry from loan application: -5 to -10 points
  • New loan account (lowers average age): -5 to -10 points

Long-term impact (3–12 months): +40 to +100 points

  • Credit card utilization drops from 80% to 0%: +30 to +80 points
  • On-time loan payments build positive history: +10 to +20 points over time
  • Credit mix improves (installment + revolving): +5 to +10 points
📈 Net result: Most borrowers see a net score increase of 30–60 points within 6 months of consolidating.

Step-by-Step: How to Consolidate Credit Card Debt

Step 1: Check Your Credit Score

Use Credit Karma (VantageScore) and Experian (FICO 8). Know where you stand before applying. See our credit building guide if your score is below 580.

Step 2: Get Soft Pull Pre-Approvals

Use the lenders listed in our best personal loans guide to check rates without hurting your score.

Step 3: Compare Total Cost (Not Just Monthly Payment)

Calculate: (Monthly payment × term) + origination fee. Choose the lowest total cost, not the lowest monthly payment.

Step 4: Accept the Best Offer (One Hard Pull)

Only formally apply with the lender you'll accept. One hard inquiry is fine.

Step 5: Use Loan Funds to Pay Cards Immediately

Don't spend the money elsewhere. Pay credit cards the same day funds arrive.

Step 6: Close or Freeze Credit Cards (Temporarily)

The #1 mistake is running cards up again. Either close them (hurts utilization) or freeze/lock them so you can't use them.

Step 7: Set Up Autopay on the Loan

Never miss a loan payment. Set autopay from your checking account.

When NOT to Use a Personal Loan for Credit Cards

🚫 If you haven't fixed the spending problem

Consolidation treats the symptom, not the cause. If you run up credit cards again after paying them off, you'll have loan debt + new card debt = double trouble.

🚫 If you can pay off cards in under 12 months anyway

Calculate: If you can pay $2,000/month toward $15,000 debt, you'll pay ~$1,500 in credit card interest. A loan with a 5% origination fee ($750) might not save enough to justify the hard inquiry.

🚫 If your credit score is below 580

You'll get offered 28–36% APR loans – not much better than credit cards. Focus on building credit first using a credit-builder loan.

FAQs

Can I use a personal loan to pay off credit cards from the same bank?

Yes, most banks allow this. However, some have restrictions. Call and ask before applying.

Will my credit cards close automatically after I pay them off?

No. You must close them yourself (or leave them open with $0 balance). Leaving them open risks running up debt again.

Should I close credit cards after paying them off with a loan?

If you have self-control, keep them open (improves utilization and age). If you struggle with spending, close them or lower limits to $500.

How many times can I consolidate debt?

Theoretically multiple times, but each loan application causes a hard inquiry. Avoid consolidating more than once every 12–18 months.

Final Recommendation

Consolidate if: Your credit card APRs are 20%+, your credit score is 620+, and you can get a personal loan at 15% or lower. You'll save thousands.

Don't consolidate if: Your credit score is below 580 (improve it first), or you haven't stopped using credit cards (you'll dig a deeper hole).
