Soft Pull vs. Hard Pull: Credit Inquiry Guide (2026)

Published: March 24, 2026 | Est. read time: 7 minutes

🔍 The short answer: Soft pulls never affect your credit score. Hard pulls drop your score 3–10 points and stay on your report for 2 years. Always use soft pulls for loan pre-approval.

Soft Pull (Soft Inquiry) – No Score Impact

A soft pull occurs when you or a company checks your credit for informational purposes. It does NOT affect your credit score and is not visible to lenders when they review your report.

Examples of soft pulls:

  • Checking your own credit score (Credit Karma, Experian, your bank)
  • Employer background checks (with your permission)
  • Pre-approved credit card offers you receive in mail
  • Loan pre-approval from lenders like Upgrade, Avant, SoFi
  • Insurance quotes
  • Landlord tenant screening (sometimes – depends on type)
✅ Always start with soft pulls. Before formally applying for any personal loan or credit card, check if the lender offers "pre-approval" or "rate check" with a soft pull only.

Hard Pull (Hard Inquiry) – Lowers Your Score

A hard pull occurs when a lender checks your credit to make a lending decision. It temporarily lowers your FICO score by 3–10 points and remains on your credit report for 2 years (but only affects your score for 12 months).

Examples of hard pulls:

  • Applying for a credit card
  • Applying for a personal loan or mortgage
  • Applying for a car loan
  • Renting an apartment (most landlords use hard pulls)
  • Opening a utility account (sometimes – depends on provider)

How Multiple Hard Inquiries Affect Your Score

Within a short period (14–45 days, depending on FICO version):

  • FICO treats multiple inquiries for the same loan type (mortgage, auto, student loan) as a single inquiry.
  • This is called "rate shopping" – designed to let you compare rates without penalty.

But for credit cards and personal loans:

  • Each application typically counts separately.
  • 3 credit card applications in one day = 3 hard pulls = 9–30 point drop.

Example of rate shopping (auto loan): You apply with 5 different lenders for a car loan within 14 days. FICO counts this as 1 hard inquiry. Your score drops 3–10 points total, not 5× that.

Example of bad behavior (credit cards): You apply for 3 different credit cards in one week. FICO counts each separately. Your score drops 15–30 points total.

When to Avoid Hard Pulls

🚫 3–6 months before a mortgage application

Mortgage lenders are sensitive to recent inquiries. Avoid any new credit applications for 6 months before applying for a home loan.

🚫 When your score is borderline (580–620)

A few hard pulls could push you below a critical threshold (e.g., 620 to 610), causing denial or higher rates.

🚫 If you have many recent inquiries (3+ in last 6 months)

Multiple inquiries signal "credit seeking behavior" to lenders, which increases perceived risk.

How to Remove Hard Inquiries (Legitimately)

You cannot remove legitimate hard inquiries early.

They stay on your report for 2 years and affect your score for 12 months. No "credit repair" company can remove legitimate hard inquiries – they're legally required to stay.

But you can dispute inaccurate inquiries:

  • You never authorized the credit check
  • The same lender pulled your credit twice by mistake
  • The inquiry is more than 2 years old (should have fallen off)

File a dispute with the credit bureau (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion). Success rate for valid disputes: 60–80%.

Real Example: $10,000 Cost of Multiple Hard Pulls

Maria's situation: Credit score 650. Needed a $15,000 personal loan for home renovation.

Bad approach: Applied to 4 different lenders without using soft pulls. Received 4 hard inquiries. Score dropped from 650 to 620. The 4th lender saw the inquiries and denied her. Eventually approved at 28% APR.

Good approach (what she should have done): Used soft pull pre-approval with Upgrade, Avant, and SoFi. Only one lender (Upgrade) offered the best rate. Formally applied only with Upgrade. One hard inquiry. Score 650 → 645. Approved at 18% APR.

Difference: On $15,000 over 3 years, 18% vs. 28% APR saves $3,200 in interest. All from avoiding unnecessary hard pulls.

FAQs

Does checking my own credit score hurt it?

No. Checking your own credit via Credit Karma, Experian, or your bank is a soft pull. No impact.

How long does a hard inquiry stay on my report?

2 years. But it only affects your FICO score for the first 12 months. After that, it remains visible but doesn't lower your score.

Can I get a hard inquiry removed if I was denied?

No. Denial doesn't remove the inquiry. The lender had permissible purpose to check your credit (your application).

Do utility companies use hard or soft pulls?

Most use soft pulls. Some use hard pulls if you have poor credit history or are requesting a deposit waiver.

How many hard pulls is "too many"?

Experts recommend no more than 2–3 hard pulls per year (excluding rate shopping for a mortgage/auto). More than 6 in 2 years is considered excessive.

Final Recommendation

  • Always start with soft pull pre-approval for any loan or credit card.
  • Only convert to a hard pull when you're confident you'll be approved and accept the offer.
  • Rate shop within 14 days for mortgages and auto loans to minimize score impact.
  • Avoid hard pulls for 6 months before a mortgage application.
